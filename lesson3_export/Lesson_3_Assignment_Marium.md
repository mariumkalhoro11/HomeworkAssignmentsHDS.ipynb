# **Task 1: Henrietta Lacks and HeLa Cells**

Henrietta Lacks was a Black woman treated for cervical cancer at Johns Hopkins Hospital in 1951. During her treatment, doctors took samples of her cancer cells without telling her or asking for her consent. These cells later became known as HeLa cells, the first human cells that could survive and reproduce indefinitely in a laboratory.

HeLa cells became extremely important for medical research and were used to develop vaccines, cancer treatments, and many scientific discoveries. However, Henrietta Lacks and her family were never informed about the use of her cells, and they did not receive any compensation or recognition for decades. This raised serious ethical concerns about consent, exploitation, and the rights of patients, especially marginalized communities.

The main ethical issues in this case include the lack of informed consent, violation of patient autonomy, and racial and social injustice. Henrietta Lacks’ cells were taken at a time when Black patients were often treated unfairly in healthcare, and her family only learned about the widespread use of her cells years later. This case shows how researchers can benefit from biological data while the individuals providing it are ignored or harmed.

To prevent this from happening, researchers should have obtained informed consent from Henrietta Lacks before collecting and using her cells. Clear policies about data and tissue ownership should be enforced, and patients should be informed about how their biological materials will be used. Additionally, ethical review boards and strict regulations can help ensure that researchers respect participants’ rights and prevent exploitation in future studies.

# **Task 2: Premera Blue Cross Data Breach**

Another major example of a health data security breach happened in 2015 and involved Premera Blue Cross, a large health insurance company in the United States. In March 2015, Premera announced that hackers had broken into its computer systems and accessed the personal information of about 11 million people. The stolen data included names, addresses, dates of birth, Social Security numbers, bank account information, and medical claim details. The cyberattack lasted for several months before it was discovered, which shows how difficult it can be for organizations to detect advanced hackers. The attackers used malware to access internal systems and copy sensitive information without being noticed. This breach exposed millions of individuals to risks such as identity theft, financial fraud, and misuse of their medical information.

The consequences of the Premera breach were serious. Premera agreed to a $74 million settlement in 2020 to compensate affected individuals and to improve its cybersecurity practices. The company also faced criticism for not having strong enough security measures in place to protect sensitive health data. This incident increased public concern about how health organizations store and protect personal data.

To prevent this breach, Premera could have implemented stronger cybersecurity controls, such as encryption, network monitoring, and regular security audits. Multi-factor authentication and employee training to detect phishing attacks could also have reduced the risk. Overall, the case shows the importance of proactive cybersecurity measures and constant monitoring to protect health data from unauthorized access.

Source: Washington State Attorney General report on the Premera breach — Attorney General investigation into Premera data breach

# **Task 3: Bias in Health Data – Race-Based Kidney Function Algorithms (eGFR)**

Another important example of bias in health data involves how kidney function has historically been measured using race-based formulas. For many years, doctors used an equation called eGFR (estimated glomerular filtration rate) to assess how well a patient’s kidneys were working. This equation included a correction factor for Black patients, assuming that Black individuals naturally have higher muscle mass than other groups. This assumption caused bias because it often overestimated kidney function in Black patients. As a result, Black patients were less likely to be diagnosed with chronic kidney disease early, referred to specialists, or placed on transplant waiting lists. This contributed to racial disparities in kidney care and outcomes.

The consequences of this bias were serious. Many Black patients experienced delayed treatment, worsening disease, and unequal access to kidney transplants. The use of race as a biological factor also reinforced stereotypes and ignored social and environmental factors that influence health. To prevent this bias, researchers and clinicians should use race-neutral algorithms and validate medical models using diverse populations. Instead of using race as a biological variable, doctors should focus on genetic, social, and environmental factors. Increased representation in clinical research and continuous evaluation of medical algorithms can help reduce bias in health data and improve health equity.

Source: New England Journal of Medicine (NEJM) — https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMoa2102953

# **Task 4: Anonymization of Health Data – COVID-19 Data Sharing by the NHS (UK)**

A real example of anonymization of health data happened during the COVID-19 pandemic when the UK National Health Service (NHS) shared large datasets with researchers to study the virus. Because the data included sensitive health information, the NHS used anonymization techniques to protect patient privacy. One major technique used was pseudonymization, where personal identifiers such as names, NHS numbers, and addresses were replaced with unique codes. This allowed researchers to track patient outcomes without knowing their identities. The NHS also used data aggregation and generalization, such as grouping patients by age ranges and regions instead of sharing exact ages or addresses. In some cases, secure research environments were used so researchers could analyze data without downloading it.

These anonymization methods helped protect individual privacy while still allowing scientists to study COVID-19 trends, treatment outcomes, and risk factors. However, there is still a risk of re-identification if anonymized data is combined with other datasets, so strong security policies and limited access were necessary. Overall, this example shows how anonymization techniques like pseudonymization and aggregation can balance privacy protection with the need for public health research.

Source: Pseudonymisation (de-identification of data) — https://www.midandsouthessex.ics.nhs.uk/your-information/pseudonymisation-de-identification-of-data/

# **Quiz**

1. What is the primary goal of data anonymization?
	- The primary goal of data anonymization is to protect individual privacy by removing or altering identifying information so that individuals cannot be re-identified, while still allowing the data to be used for research and analysis.

2. What is the Havasupai Tribe case about?
	- The Havasupai Tribe case involves researchers using blood samples from tribe members for genetic studies beyond what participants originally consented to, including research on mental health and ancestry. The case highlighted ethical issues related to informed consent and misuse of Indigenous genetic data.

3. What was the largest health data breach in history?
	- One of the largest health data breaches occurred in 2015 when Anthem Inc. was hacked, exposing the personal and health information of approximately 78 million individuals.

4. What is the pulse oximeter controversy about?
	- The pulse oximeter controversy refers to evidence that these devices are less accurate for individuals with darker skin tones, which can lead to undetected low oxygen levels and contribute to health disparities.

5. What is the role of an Institutional Review Board (IRB) in research?
	- An Institutional Review Board (IRB) reviews research involving human subjects to ensure ethical standards are met, risks are minimized, informed consent is obtained, and participants’ rights and welfare are protected.

6. What is the difference between spreadsheet software and database software for health data management?
	- Spreadsheet software is designed for small-scale data entry and analysis, while database software is built to handle large, complex datasets, support multiple users, enforce data integrity, and provide stronger security and query capabilities.

7. What is a conflict of interest in public health research?
	- A conflict of interest occurs when a researcher’s personal, financial, or professional interests could influence, or appear to influence, the design, conduct, or interpretation of research.

8. What is informed consent in the context of health data collection?
	- Informed consent is the process of providing individuals with clear information about how their health data will be collected, used, stored, and shared, and obtaining their voluntary agreement to participate.

9. What is data masking in the context of de-identification of health data?
	- Data masking is a technique that replaces real personal identifiers (such as names or social security numbers) with altered or fictitious values to prevent identification of individuals.

10. What is the principle of k-anonymity in de-identification?
 	 - K-anonymity is a privacy model where each individual’s data cannot be distinguished from at least k–1 other individuals in the dataset, reducing the risk of re-identification.

11. What is the main concern about conflicts of interest in public health research?
 	 - The main concern is that conflicts of interest can bias research findings, compromise scientific integrity, and undermine public trust in public health evidence and recommendations.

12. What is the first step in managing conflicts of interest in public health research?
 	 - The first step is disclosure, where researchers openly report any financial, personal, or professional relationships that could influence their work.

13. What is the role of independent oversight in managing conflicts of interest?
 	 - Independent oversight bodies (such as ethics committees or regulatory agencies) monitor research to ensure conflicts of interest are appropriately managed and do not compromise research integrity.

14. What is one strategy for mitigating conflicts of interest in public health research?
 	 - One strategy is implementing transparency policies, such as restricting funding sources, separating financial interests from research decisions, or requiring external peer review.

15. What is the potential impact of not properly managing conflicts of interest in public health research?
 	 - Failure to manage conflicts of interest can lead to biased results, harmful public health policies, loss of credibility, and reduced public trust in scientific research and institutions.
